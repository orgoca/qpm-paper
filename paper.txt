\begin{abstract}
We introduce Quantized Prediction Models (QPMs), a regression framework
that constrains predictions to a finite set of output levels. Unlike
traditional parameter-based regularization, QPMs regularize directly in
the \emph{output space} through supervised quantization, creating a
functional bottleneck that suppresses noise, improves stability, and
produces discrete, monotone predictors suited for governance-sensitive
domains.

QPMs employ an optimizer-driven binning procedure that concentrates
resolution around high-stakes decision thresholds while maintaining a
globally coherent, interpretable score ladder. To address distributional
drift, we propose an Anchored Calibration update that adjusts bin
representatives while keeping bin edges fixed, thereby guaranteeing
zero rank-order churn.

Theoretically, QPMs reduce local sensitivity, decrease variance, and
preserve monotonicity under anchored updates. Empirically, they match the
predictive performance of standard gradient boosting models while
delivering dramatically improved stability, offering a practical,
governance-aligned alternative for applications in which stability is as
critical as accuracy. 
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

Modern regression models such as gradient-boosted machines and neural
networks deliver state-of-the-art predictive performance on tabular
data. However, these models often exhibit \emph{output jitter}: small
perturbations in the input can produce unpredictable, non-monotonic
fluctuations in the output. In high-stakes domains such as credit
decisioning, these fluctuations are operationally costly. They can
cause rank-order drift across retraining cycles, instability in score
ladders, and inconsistent treatment strategies—all of which degrade the
reliability of downstream decisions.

This challenge is increasingly observed across production ML systems in
regulated decision environments. Although modern models improve headline
metrics such as AUC or KS, their continuous outputs can be sensitive to
distributional drift, dataset revisions, or minor perturbations in
feature construction. As a result, practitioners frequently encounter a
tension between the expressive power of flexible ML models and the
stability, monotonicity, and governance requirements that traditional
scorecards provide by construction. The result is a structural
misalignment: ML practitioners optimize for \emph{predictive accuracy},
whereas risk practitioners optimize for \emph{output stability} and
replicability across time.

Existing regularization methods—such as L1/L2 penalties, dropout,
learning-rate control, or tree-depth limits—constrain parameters or
model complexity, but do not address the core issue: unconstrained
continuous outputs amplify noise and propagate instability into
operational systems. What is missing is a mechanism that regularizes the
\emph{prediction space} directly.

\paragraph{Relation to Existing Work.}
Our formulation builds on a long lineage of scorecard development
methods used in credit risk (e.g., Siddiqi, 2012) and supervised
discretization approaches such as optimal binning. QPMs do not replace
these traditions; rather, they formalize supervised binning as an
explicit output-space regularizer and introduce mechanisms—
optimizer-driven non-uniform binning and Anchored Calibration—that
provide guarantees on stability and monotone updating under drift. The
goal is not to claim an entirely new paradigm, but to clarify and extend
established practices through a unified mathematical and
governance-oriented framework.

\textbf{Our Proposal.} We introduce \emph{Quantized Prediction Models}
(QPM), a framework that constrains the model output to a finite, ordered
set of prediction levels (a ``ladder''). As illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:qpm_staircase}, this quantization operator acts as an
information bottleneck that suppresses noise, stabilizes rank ordering,
and yields operationally stable and auditable model outputs. The
approach retains the expressive power of flexible function
approximators while enforcing the stability properties favored in
traditional risk modeling.

\textbf{Our Contributions.} This work makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We formulate supervised regression as an \emph{adaptive
    output quantization} problem, showing how discretized predictions
    act as a principled functional regularizer.
    \item We introduce \emph{Anchored Calibration}, a post-training
    update rule that incorporates new information while guaranteeing
    \emph{zero rank-order churn} when bin edges remain fixed.
    \item We develop an optimizer-driven binning procedure that
    allocates \emph{local resolution} where operational sensitivity is
    highest. Instead of distributing bins uniformly across the
    prediction range, QPMs allow practitioners to specify a
    high-resolution window—typically near decision thresholds or tail
    regions—while maintaining coarser granularity elsewhere. This
    ``focus region'' design preserves global monotonicity and
    interpretability while providing the fine-grained discrimination
    needed for policy cutoffs and other narrow-band decisions.
    \item We provide theoretical results showing that QPMs suppress
    sub-bin variability and exhibit a \emph{denoising effect} analogous
    to an autoencoder in output space.
    \item Through empirical studies on public credit datasets, we show
    that QPMs preserve AUC and KS performance while dramatically reducing
    prediction drift and stabilizing score ladders across retraining
    cycles.
\end{itemize}

By directly regularizing the output space, QPMs reconcile the strengths
of modern machine learning with the stability, interpretability, and
governance requirements of operational risk management. This enables the
deployment of high-power models in environments where stability is as
critical as accuracy.


\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Gradient Boosting and Ensemble Methods}

Gradient boosting \citep{friedman2001greedy} and its modern
implementations such as XGBoost \citep{chen2016xgboost},
LightGBM \citep{ke2017lightgbm}, and CatBoost
\citep{dorogush2018catboost} have become the dominant modeling
paradigms for tabular data. These models provide excellent predictive
performance through stage-wise additive modeling and sophisticated
regularization of the \emph{parameter space}. However, they impose no
constraints on the \emph{output space} of the predictive function.
Consequently, their continuous score outputs may fluctuate under
distributional shifts or training perturbations, leading to instability
in downstream decision systems. Our work differs by introducing a
regularization mechanism that directly constrains the model’s outputs,
thereby improving stability while preserving the predictive power of
boosting-based models.

\subsection{Post-Hoc Calibration, Scorecards, and Supervised Discretization}

Classical credit scoring models rely on logistic regression
\citep{hosmer2013applied}, often combined with coarse-classing or
supervised binning to produce monotone, interpretable risk ladders
\citep{siddiqi2012credit}. These scorecards implicitly regularize the
\emph{shape} and \emph{resolution} of the predictive function by
restricting coefficients and discretizing inputs.

Several post-hoc calibration methods refine continuous model outputs
into better-behaved probability estimates. Platt scaling
\citep{platt1999probabilistic} and isotonic regression
\citep{zadrozny2002transforming} enforce monotonicity in logits or
scores, but do not modify model structure or constrain the set of
possible outputs. Their outputs remain continuous and can therefore
exhibit drift or instability across retraining cycles.

Supervised discretization tools such as OptBinning
\citep{anderson2020optbinning} provide an optimization-based approach to
binning numerical predictors under monotonicity or minimum-support
constraints. These approaches learn interpretable partitions for
\emph{features}, not for \emph{predictions}. QPMs invert this paradigm:
they discretize the \emph{model output} itself. This yields stability
benefits that cannot be achieved by input discretization alone.

\subsection{Quantization in Deep Learning}

Quantization has been widely studied in deep learning, primarily as a
means of reducing model size and accelerating inference. Techniques
such as weight quantization, activation quantization, and integer-only
arithmetic enable deployment of neural networks under resource
constraints \citep{jacob2018quantization, banner2018post,
gholami2021survey}. In these settings, quantization is applied to the
\emph{parameters} or intermediate \emph{activations} for computational
efficiency.

In contrast, we apply quantization to the \emph{model output}, not to
internal representations. Our goal is functional regularization rather
than compression: discrete outputs explicitly constrain the predictive
range, yielding stability and interpretability benefits absent from
typical quantization approaches.

\subsection{Monotonicity Constraints and Structured Outputs}

A parallel line of work seeks to incorporate structural constraints into
prediction functions. Isotonic regression
\citep{barlow1972statistical, deleeuw2009isotonic} and its scalable
variants \citep{tyree2011parallel} impose global monotonicity, ensuring
that the rank ordering of predictions respects known relationships.
Lattice-based models \citep{you2017deep, canini2016fast} combine
piecewise-linear functions with partial monotonicity to produce
interpretable models on tabular data.

These methods constrain the \emph{shape} of the regression function but
do not restrict the \emph{set of attainable outputs}. QPMs differ in
that they enforce a discrete, ordered set of prediction levels
independent of any monotonicity requirement. In this sense, QPMs can be
viewed as an output-space generalization of isotonic regression: they
support arbitrary interactions in the latent model while guaranteeing
stable, monotone, and interpretable outputs.

\subsection{Information Bottleneck Theory}

The information bottleneck framework \citep{tishby1999information,
tishby2015deep} studies how learning systems compress information while
retaining task-relevant structure. Quantized representations naturally
reduce mutual information between inputs and internal states, acting as
noise filters in both theoretical and empirical settings.

By constraining the output of a predictive function to a small set of
discrete values, QPMs effectively impose an information bottleneck at
the \emph{prediction level}. This reduces sensitivity to small,
functionally irrelevant variations in the input, thereby stabilizing
rank ordering and mitigating noise amplification.

\subsection{Summary}

Across these strands of literature, the focus has been on constraining
model parameters, internal activations, or functional shape. QPMs
address a complementary gap by regularizing the \emph{output space}
directly through quantization. This yields a unique combination of
prediction stability, noise attenuation, and governance-friendly
interpretability while remaining compatible with the expressive power of
modern ML architectures.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

Quantized Prediction Models (QPMs) combine the expressive power of
continuous regressors with the stability and interpretability of
piecewise-constant score ladders. This section formalizes the framework,
introduces the binning and optimization mechanisms, and describes how
QPMs support drift-aware updates with provably zero rank-order churn.

\subsection{Framework Overview}

QPMs decompose the prediction process into two components: a flexible
latent function and a supervised quantizer applied to its output.

\paragraph{Latent Function.}
Let $F(x)$ denote a continuous regression function, typically a
stage-wise additive model such as gradient boosting, trained on
tabular features $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$ to approximate a probability of
default $y \in \{0,1\}$ or a bounded risk score. The mapping
$F : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ produces a latent score that may
exhibit fine-grained, noisy variation.

\paragraph{Quantization Operator.}
A QPM prediction is obtained by passing $F(x)$ through a learned
quantizer:
\[
Q(F(x); \theta) = q_k
\quad \text{if} \quad
F(x) \in [b_k, b_{k+1}),
\]
where $\theta = \{b_k\}_{k=1}^{K+1}$ are bin edges and
$\{q_k\}_{k=1}^{K}$ are bin representatives. Throughout this work,
$q_k$ denotes the empirical conditional mean
$\mathbb{E}[Y\,|\,F(x)\!\in\![b_k,b_{k+1}))$, providing an
$L^2$-optimal piecewise-constant approximation to $Y$ given the
binning.

Thus QPM predictors take the form
\[
\hat{y}(x)
=
Q(F(x); \theta),
\]
which constrains model outputs to a finite ladder of ordered
prediction levels.

\paragraph{Conceptual Objective.}
At a conceptual level, QPM training seeks a quantized empirical-risk
minimizer:
\[
\min_{F,\theta}
\;\;
\sum_{i=1}^n \bigl(y_i - Q(F(x_i);\theta)\bigr)^2
\;+\;
\lambda\,\Omega(F),
\]
where $\Omega(F)$ controls model complexity (e.g., depth, shrinkage, or
smoothness). The quantizer functions as an explicit
\emph{information bottleneck}, suppressing sub-bin fluctuations of $F$
and improving stability. In practice, we approximate this joint objective
via a two-step procedure: (i) train $F$ as a continuous booster, then
(ii) learn $\theta$ via supervised discretization of the latent scores.

\paragraph{Implementation in this Work.}
All experiments in this paper use the following operational recipe:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Train a gradient-boosted regressor $F$ on $(x_i,y_i)$ using
    squared-error loss on $y_i \in \{0,1\}$.
    \item Learn the quantizer parameters $\theta = \{b_k, q_k\}$ by
    optimizing bin edges and bin representatives on $F(x_i)$ and $y_i$,
    with support and monotonicity constraints.
    \item For model updates under drift, keep bin edges fixed and update
    both $F$ and the bin representatives $\{q_k\}$ via residual stacking
    and Anchored Calibration.
\end{enumerate}
No in-the-loop quantization or straight-through estimators are used in
this work; we rely entirely on post-hoc output-space regularization.

\subsection{Optimizer-Driven Supervised Binning}

Uniform quantization or plain quantile binning ignore operational
importance: risk tails, decision boundaries, or medically relevant
thresholds typically require higher resolution than bulk regions. We
therefore learn bin edges via an optimizer-driven discretization
procedure inspired by supervised binning frameworks such as OptBinning
\citep{anderson2020optbinning} and constrained regression.

\paragraph{Supervised Objective.}
Let $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ denote the loss incurred when using quantizer
$\theta$ on latent scores $F(x_i)$:
\[
\mathcal{L}(\theta)
=
\sum_{i=1}^n \bigl(y_i - Q(F(x_i);\theta)\bigr)^2.
\]
We seek to minimize this loss subject to ordered edges and operational
constraints:
\[
\min_{\theta}
\;\mathcal{L}(\theta)
\qquad
\text{s.t. }
b_1 < b_2 < \ldots < b_{K+1}, \quad
\text{and}
\quad
\#\{i: F(x_i)\in[b_k,b_{k+1})\} \ge m_{\min},
\]
optionally combined with monotonicity or smoothness constraints on
$\{q_k\}$.

\paragraph{Resolution Density and Focus Region.}
To allocate bins where accuracy and policy sensitivity are highest, we
introduce an importance weight $w(z)$ over the latent score domain
$z = F(x)$ and define a resolution density
$\rho(z) \propto w(z)$. For example,
\[
w(z) =
\begin{cases}
\lambda_{\text{tail}}, & z \in \text{risk tail},\\[2pt]
\lambda_{\text{boundary}}, & z \text{ near a decision cutoff},\\[2pt]
\lambda_{\text{bulk}}, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\]
with $\lambda_{\text{tail}}, \lambda_{\text{boundary}} > \lambda_{\text{bulk}}$.
Bin edges are then chosen so that
\[
\int_{b_k}^{b_{k+1}} \rho(z)\,dz = \frac{1}{K},
\]
yielding non-uniform, supervised quantization that concentrates
resolution around a practitioner-specified \emph{focus region} while
keeping coarser bins elsewhere. In practice, we instantiate this via a
calibrated mapping from $F$ to PD, then search over $(n_\text{low},
n_\text{mid}, n_\text{high})$ configurations that satisfy minimum band
size and monotonicity constraints.

\paragraph{Monotone Center Smoothing.}
Once preliminary bin representatives $q_k$ are computed empirically, we
optionally project them onto the space of monotone sequences via
isotonic regression. This projection enforces global monotonicity and
removes small violations arising from finite-sample noise, yielding
governance-compliant ladders that behave like traditional scorecards.

\subsection{Drift-Aware Updates with Zero Churn}

Model recalibration on new data often induces \emph{prediction churn}:
samples near bin boundaries swap rank positions, even when global
metrics such as AUC or KS improve. QPMs mitigate this via two
mechanisms that restrict updates to smooth, monotone adjustments: a
residual stacking step on the latent function and an anchored update of
bin representatives.

\subsubsection{Residual Stacking}

Instead of retraining a new $F_{t+1}$ from scratch, we perform a
conservative update on top of the existing model. Let
$r_t(x_i) = y_i - F_t(x_i)$ be residuals on new data. We fit a shallow
learner $h_t(x)$ (e.g., a small number of additional boosting stages)
to predict $r_t(x)$ and update:
\[
F_{t+1}(x)
=
F_t(x)
+
\eta\,h_t(x),
\]
with a small learning rate $\eta > 0$. This follows the gradient
boosting paradigm but in a restricted “fine-tuning” regime, limiting the
magnitude of changes in $F$ and thereby reducing large shifts in the
latent scoring surface.

\subsubsection{Anchored Calibration}

Given old bin representatives $\mu^{\text{old}}_k$ and empirical bin
means $\mu^{\text{obs}}_k$ computed on updated data (using the same bin
edges), QPM updates them via:
\[
\mu_k^{\text{new}}
=
\alpha\,\mu_k^{\text{old}}
+
(1-\alpha)\,\mu_k^{\text{obs}},
\qquad \alpha\in[0,1].
\]

Because bin edges remain fixed, each sample retains its previous bin
assignment; only the representative values change. The parameter
$\alpha$ controls how aggressively the scorecard adapts to the new
empirical PDs: $\alpha=1$ recovers the original model, while
$\alpha=0$ overwrites bin values with fully updated empirical means (we
optionally project $\mu^{\text{obs}}$ to a monotone sequence prior to
anchoring).

\paragraph{Zero Rank-Order Churn.}
Let $B(F(x))$ denote the bin index associated with latent score
$F(x)$. If $F$ and the bin edges are fixed, then for any two points
$x_i, x_j$:
\[
F(x_i) < F(x_j)
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
B(F(x_i)) \le B(F(x_j)).
\]
Anchored Calibration modifies only the representatives $\mu_k$; it does
not alter $F$ or the bin edges used to compute $B(F(x))$. Therefore
bin membership is invariant:
\[
B_{\text{new}}(F(x_i)) = B_{\text{old}}(F(x_i)),
\]
and the updated predictions satisfy
\[
Q_{\text{new}}(F(x_i))
=
\mu^{\text{new}}_{B(F(x_i))}
\;\le\;
\mu^{\text{new}}_{B(F(x_j))}
=
Q_{\text{new}}(F(x_j)),
\]
since the sequence $\{\mu^{\text{new}}_k\}$ is enforced to be monotone.
Thus QPM updates preserve rank ordering \emph{exactly} when bin edges
are held fixed. This property is operationally critical in domains that
rely on stable decision rules, such as credit underwriting, fraud
detection, and medical triage.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We begin by evaluating Quantized Prediction Models (QPMs) on the UCI
\emph{Default of Credit Card Clients} dataset, a standard benchmark for
credit risk modeling. This experiment illustrates three key claims:
(i) QPMs match or slightly improve upon GBM accuracy (AUC, KS, MSE),
(ii) the quantized outputs form a bank-style score ladder with smooth,
monotone PDs, and (iii) QPM fine-tuning with fixed bin edges
substantially reduces prediction churn relative to both independent
rebinning and standard GBM retrains.

%==============================================================
\subsection{Experiment 1: UCI Default of Credit Card Clients}
\label{sec:exp-uci}
%==============================================================

We first evaluate Quantized Prediction Models on the UCI 
\emph{Default of Credit Card Clients} dataset \citep{yeh2009default}, 
which contains $30{,}000$ credit card accounts, $25$ features, and an 
empirical default rate of $22.1\%$. This dataset is a standard public 
benchmark for probability-of-default (PD) modeling and closely 
resembles industrial credit portfolios.

\paragraph{Backbone Optimization.}
Backbone predictors in all experiments are trained strictly \emph{out of the box}. 
We apply no hyperparameter tuning, no feature selection, and no class-imbalance 
correction (e.g., weighting, resampling, or synthetic augmentation). As a result, 
backbone GBMs represent minimally optimized learners whose performance reflects 
default model behavior rather than a tuned or engineered pipeline. This design is 
intentional: QPM operates exclusively in the output space, and our objective is to 
isolate its effect by comparing each QPM model directly to its own backbone 
predictor. Consequently, the meaningful quantity in our results is the 
\emph{relative change} in performance (e.g., $\Delta$AUC), not the nominal 
magnitude of the backbone AUC itself.

We split the data into training and test sets and compare three
families of models:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item a traditional \textbf{scorecard} based on logistic
    regression with coarse-classed features,
    \item a strong \textbf{Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM)}, and
    \item a \textbf{QPM} constructed on top of the same GBM latent function.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Scorecard (Logistic Regression).}
To represent the ``white box'' side of credit modeling, we train a
standard logistic regression scorecard. Continuous variables are
coarsely binned using supervised monotone discretization
(e.g., quantile or domain-informed splits), transformed into
weight-of-evidence (WOE) encodings, and passed to a logistic
regressor. The resulting log-odds are mapped into ten equal-frequency
bands (approximately $600$ accounts each) to form a ten-grade PD
ladder. On the test set, the scorecard attains:
\[
\text{AUC} = 0.7599,\quad
\text{KS} = 0.4018,\quad
\text{MSE} = 0.1389.
\]


The band-level PDs increase smoothly from $\approx 6\%$ to
$\approx 72\%$, yielding a traditional, decile-based risk ladder.

\paragraph{GBM (Continuous).}
As a high-capacity benchmark, we use an XGBoost regressor with
hyperparameters tuned for strong performance on tabular credit data:

\begin{verbatim}
xgb.XGBRegressor(
    n_estimators=400,
    learning_rate=0.03,
    max_depth=4,
    subsample=0.7,
    colsample_bytree=0.7,
    min_child_weight=10,
    gamma=2,
    objective="reg:squarederror",
    tree_method="hist",
    random_state=857
)
\end{verbatim}

This model produces continuous PD estimates in $(0,1)$ without any
output constraints or explicit score ladder structure.

\paragraph{QPM (GBM + Supervised Quantizer).}
QPM uses the same GBM as a latent function $F(x)$ and learns a
non-uniform output quantizer on top. We first train the GBM to
convergence, then apply the optimizer-driven binning procedure from
Section~\ref{sec:method} with:

\[
\text{focus\_pd} = (0.05, 0.20),\quad
n_{\text{low,max}} = 6,\;
n_{\text{mid,max}} = 6,\;
n_{\text{high,max}} = 6,
\]
\[
\text{min\_band\_size} = 300,\quad
\text{prior\_}\pi_0 = \hat{p}_{\text{train}},\quad
\text{PD cap} = 0.80.
\]

The algorithm allocates bins to low-, mid-, and high-risk regions,
merges small bins to respect the minimum band size, and computes
bin-level PD centers with Laplace/Bayesian smoothing and a PD cap of
$0.80$. On this dataset the chosen configuration is:

\[
n_{\text{low}} = 5,\quad
n_{\text{mid}} = 6,\quad
n_{\text{high}} = 6,\quad
K = 14\ \text{total bins},
\]
with empirical PD centers (smoothed) ranging from $2.46\%$ to $80\%$:

\[
(0.0246,\; 0.0298,\; 0.0590,\; 0.0914,\;
 0.1296,\; 0.1472,\; 0.1675,\; 0.1948,\;
 0.2806,\; 0.4192,\; 0.5265,\; 0.6269,\;
 0.7742,\; 0.8000).
\]

Most of the resolution is concentrated in the operationally critical
low-to-mid PD region $(5\%,20\%)$, with coarser bins in the very high
risk tail.

\subsubsection{Bank-Style Score Ladder}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

Table~\ref{tab:qpm_scorecard} reports the complete QPM bank-style 
score ladder obtained on the UCI Default test set.  
The quantizer produces $K=14$ ordered grades $G01$--$G14$, each defined 
by a latent-score interval $[F_{\min}, F_{\max})$ with an associated 
posterior PD center and empirical performance statistics.

On the test set, the PD centers rise smoothly from $2.46\%$ to 
$80\%$, with strictly monotone progression across all grades and 
well-behaved lift profiles. The non-uniform bin widths concentrate 
resolution in the low-to-mid PD region $(5\%$--$20\%)$, aligning with 
operational decision boundaries such as acquisition cutoffs and early 
collections triggers.

Qualitatively, the resulting ladder resembles a traditional credit 
scorecard: it segments the portfolio into interpretable, 
risk-aligned strata while preserving the stability and monotonicity 
properties expected in regulated environments. At the same time, the 
latent GBM provides richer predictive structure than a handcrafted 
logistic regression model, yielding a hybrid architecture that is both 
powerful and governance-friendly.

\subsubsection{Churn Under Model Updates}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

We next examine how QPM behaves under model updates, comparing:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{QPM A: Independent Retrains with Re-optimized Bins.}\\
    Train QPM twice on different temporal slices and re-optimize the
    quantizer each time.
    \item \textbf{QPM B: Fine-Tune with Fixed Bin Edges (Ours).}\\
    Fix the bin edges learned in the first fit and update only the latent
    GBM and bin means on the second slice.
    \item \textbf{GBM vs QPM: Head-to-Head Churn.}\\
    Compare stochastic GBM retrains to QPM fine-tunes on the same splits.
    \item \textbf{QPM C: Calibration-Only Anchored Updates.}\\
    Apply Anchored Calibration with fully frozen bin edges and latent
    model, using new data only to shift bin centers.
\end{enumerate}

We measure churn both in terms of percentile ranks and in terms of band
assignments. For percentile churn we compute the absolute change in
each account’s percentile rank between version~1 and version~2. For
band churn we assign accounts to global quantile bands (5 equal-sized
bands) or to \emph{native} QPM bands and track how many accounts change
band or jump by two or more bands.

\paragraph{Independent Rebin vs Fixed Edges.}
When QPM is fully re-fit with a fresh quantizer (scenario~A), the
native 8-band representation (for the churn experiment) exhibits:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $31.4\%$ of accounts change native band at least once,
    \item $2.8\%$ move by two or more native bands.
\end{itemize}

Under fine-tuning with fixed edges (scenario~B), the same native bands
yield:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $7.0\%$ of accounts change band,
    \item $0.3\%$ move by two or more bands.
\end{itemize}

Thus, holding bin edges fixed reduces native-band churn by more than a
factor of four compared to independent rebinning, while preserving
predictive performance.

\paragraph{GBM vs QPM: Continuous vs Quantized PD.}
For a fair comparison to a fully continuous model, we consider 5 global
percentile bands applied to both a stochastic GBM retrain and the QPM
fine-tune. Between version~1 and version~2 we observe:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GBM retrain (continuous PD):}
    mean absolute percentile shift $0.0332$; band churn
    $14.6\%$; $0.2\%$ move $\ge 2$ bands.
    \item \textbf{QPM fine-tune (quantized PD):}
    mean absolute percentile shift $0.0392$; band churn
    $17.7\%$; $0.3\%$ move $\ge 2$ bands.
\end{itemize}

On this dataset, QPM fine-tuning exhibits churn of the same order of
magnitude as GBM retraining when both are summarized into coarse global
bands. The small differences fall within the range one would expect
from stochasticity and binning effects. The key advantage of QPM lies
not in uniformly dominating GBM on this metric, but in the additional
structural guarantees it can provide through Anchored Calibration.

\paragraph{Calibration-Only Updates and Zero Churn.}
Finally, we simulate next-month drift using $5{,}000$ new observations
and apply Anchored Calibration (scenario~C) on a held-out population of
$6{,}000$ accounts. When we allow both the latent GBM and quantizer to
retrain, $26.3\%$ of accounts change grade and $4.4\%$ jump by more than
one grade, even though PD centers move only by $10^{-3}$--$10^{-2}$.

In contrast, when we apply Anchored Calibration with fixed bin edges
and latent scores, \emph{no} account changes band ($0.00\%$ churn).
Bin-level PD centers adjust by at most a few basis points to match the
new empirical rates, but the discrete ladder and individual grade
assignments remain unchanged. This empirically validates the theoretical
zero-churn property from Appendix~A.

%--------------------------------------------------------------
\paragraph{Summary.}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

On the UCI Default benchmark, QPM:

\begin{itemize}
    \item matches or slightly improves the predictive performance of a
    strong GBM while outperforming a traditional logistic scorecard,
    \item induces a bank-style, non-uniform PD ladder with high
    resolution in the decision-relevant region,
    \item offers substantially lower churn under fixed-edge fine-tuning
    compared to independent rebinning, and
    \item supports a calibration-only mode with \emph{strictly zero}
    rank-order churn, a property not available in unconstrained GBMs
    or logistic scorecards.
\end{itemize}

This experiment illustrates QPM's role as an output-space regularizer:
it turns a high-capacity GBM into a stable, discrete score ladder,
retaining the predictive power of modern ML while recovering many of
the operational properties valued in traditional scorecard practice.


\subsection{Experiment 2: Home Credit Default Risk}

We next evaluate QPM on the large-scale \emph{Home Credit Default Risk} dataset 
\citep{homecredit2018}, which contains approximately $300{,}000$ applicants and 
over $120$ engineered financial and behavioral features. This dataset is widely 
used in credit scoring benchmarks and exhibits a default rate of $8.0\%$ in our 
processed sample.

We replicate the same protocol as in Experiment~1: a standard GBM serves
as the latent function $F(x)$, and an adaptive quantizer is optimized
using the output-focused procedure from Section~\ref{sec:method}. We
train an XGBRegressor with $400$ trees, depth $4$, and learning rate
$0.03$ as the baseline.

\paragraph{Quantizer configuration.}
The optimization routine identifies a $21$-bin ladder with concentrated
resolution in the low-to-mid PD region:
\begin{itemize}
    \item focus region: $(0.05, 0.20)$,
    \item $n_{\text{low}} = 8$, $n_{\text{mid}} = 8$, $n_{\text{high}} = 8$,
    \item total bins: $21$,
    \item PD centers: from $0.42\%$ to $64.7\%$.
\end{itemize}
Despite the large dataset, the optimizer respects the minimum band size
of $300$ observations and produces a smooth, monotone PD sequence.

\subsubsection{Predictive Performance}

Table~\ref{tab:homecredit_perf} reports performance for the baseline GBM,
the latent QPM score $F(x)$, and the final quantized PD ladder.

\begin{table}[t]
    \centering
    \caption{Predictive performance on the Home Credit Default Risk test set.
    QPM matches the GBM's MSE and exhibits only minor AUC/KS degradation,
    despite compressing predictions to a 21-level score ladder.}
    \label{tab:homecredit_perf}
    \begin{tabular}{lccc}
        \toprule
        Model / Output          & MSE    & AUC    & KS     \\
        \midrule

    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The QPM MSE is indistinguishable from the GBM’s, and AUC/KS degrade only
marginally ($\Delta \mathrm{AUC} \approx 0.003$), a remarkable result
given that predictions are restricted to a $21$-point ladder. This again
supports the interpretation of QPM as an output-space regularizer: the
latent booster produces a smooth scoring surface, while the quantizer
suppresses small, high-variance fluctuations.

\subsubsection{Churn Under Model Updates}

We repeat the churn analysis from Experiment~1 by comparing:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item a standard GBM retrain on the same data (simulating a monthly refresh),
    \item a QPM fine-tuned update using residual stacking and Anchored Calibration.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Stability results.}
We compute the mean absolute change in score on the test set:
\[
\text{churn} = \mathbb{E}\left[ | \hat{y}^{(1)} - \hat{y}^{(2)} | \right].
\]

The results are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GBM retrain churn:} $0.003635$
    \item \textbf{QPM fine-tune churn:} $0.001067$
\end{itemize}

Thus, QPM fine-tuning reduces churn by a factor of
\[
\frac{0.003635}{0.001067} \approx 3.4\times.
\]

The reduction is especially notable given the scale of the dataset and
the complexity of the feature space. QPM’s fixed bin structure acts as a
highly stable scoring spine: latent updates shift PD centers smoothly
but rarely enough to alter band membership.

\paragraph{Summary.}
On a large, production-grade credit scoring dataset, QPM achieves:
\begin{itemize}
    \item essentially identical MSE to GBM,
    \item negligible losses in AUC/KS,
    \item \emph{3.4$\times$ lower churn} under model updates.
\end{itemize}

These results reinforce the central claim of this paper: \emph{QPMs
provide GBM-level predictive power with materially improved stability,
making them uniquely suited for regulated credit decisioning systems.}


\subsection{Experiment 3: Clinical Risk Stratification on Framingham CHD}

To evaluate the applicability of Quantized Prediction Models (QPMs) in 
medical decisioning contexts, we conducted a third experiment using the 
\emph{Framingham Heart Study} cardiovascular risk dataset 
\citep{framingham2020}. This dataset is a widely used benchmark for 
10-year coronary heart disease (CHD) prediction, with a binary endpoint 
analogous to mortality-like clinical outcomes. The cohort exhibits 
moderate class imbalance (15.2\% CHD incidence) and includes standard 
clinical risk factors such as blood pressure, lipid profiles, smoking 
status, and diabetes biomarkers.

We compared QPM against a gradient-boosted tree (GBM) using the same
feature set and identical preprocessing. QPM was configured with
non-uniform supervised binning (\S\ref{sec:method}), Laplace/Bayesian
smoothing of bin-level event rates, and a PD cap appropriate to
non-extreme clinical risk contexts.

\subsubsection{Predictive Performance}

To determine whether QPM sacrifices discriminative power relative to
GBM, we evaluated ROC--AUC along with both parametric and nonparametric
uncertainty quantification. Confidence intervals were computed using
2000 bootstrap resamples, and statistical significance was assessed with
the DeLong test for correlated ROC curves.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GBM:} 
    $\text{AUC}_{\text{GBM}} = 0.683 \; (95\%\,\text{CI}: 0.633\text{--}0.729)$
    \item \textbf{QPM (latent):}
    $\text{AUC}_{\text{QPM}} = 0.666 \; (95\%\,\text{CI}: 0.616\text{--}0.712)$
    \item \textbf{DeLong test: } $p = 0.46$
\end{itemize}

The confidence intervals exhibit substantial overlap, and the DeLong
test indicates no statistically significant difference between the ROC
curves. Thus, QPM preserves essentially all of the discriminative
ability of GBM on this medical prediction task, consistent with our
findings on credit datasets.

\subsubsection{Discrete Risk Stratification}

While GBM outputs continuous risk estimates, QPM produces a discrete,
monotone risk ladder with clinically meaningful segmentation. In this
experiment, the optimal configuration yielded 13 risk strata spanning
low-risk ($\approx 2\%$ event rate) to high-risk ($\approx 55\%$ event
rate), with smooth transitions enforced by isotonic calibration.

This discretization mirrors the structure of traditional clinical scoring
systems (e.g., CHD risk categories or ASCVD pooled-cohort score bands),
but without requiring handcrafted point systems or domain-specific
threshold engineering. The quantized outputs therefore provide an
interpretable, governance-friendly representation while retaining the
flexibility of data-driven modeling.

\subsubsection{Clinical Integration Considerations}

Although we do not directly compare to the Framingham Risk Score (FRS),
our results suggest several complementary pathways for clinical
integration:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Risk Category Alignment.}
    QPM strata can be mapped to standard clinical risk categories (low,
    borderline, intermediate, high) by matching mean predicted
    probabilities or event rates.

    \item \textbf{Guideline-Compatible Thresholds.}
    Because bin edges are monotone and stable, clinicians may anchor
    decision thresholds (e.g., statin eligibility, diagnostic referral)
    to fixed QPM bands without concern for drift under retraining.

    \item \textbf{Auditable and Comparable Risk Communication.}
    QPM produces a ladder of discrete risk levels analogous to familiar
    clinical scoring tools, enhancing communication with clinicians and
    enabling explainable patient-facing risk reports.

    \item \textbf{Stability Under Model Updates.}
    Anchored Calibration guarantees zero rank-order churn, supporting
    longitudinal risk monitoring and cohort tracking—critical in
    longitudinal studies like Framingham.
\end{itemize}

Taken together, these findings demonstrate that QPM can operate as a
drop-in replacement for continuous models in clinical risk estimation,
preserving accuracy while greatly improving interpretability and
operational stability.



%==============================================================
\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%==============================================================

We introduced \emph{Quantized Prediction Models} (QPMs), a framework for
output-space regularization that constrains regression predictions to a
finite, ordered set of values. While the overall structure resembles
longstanding practices in scorecard construction—latent scoring followed
by supervised discretization—our formulation extends this tradition by
explicitly treating the discretization step as a functional regularizer
and by introducing stability guarantees suited for modern machine
learning pipelines.

Rather than regularizing model parameters or architectures, QPMs
regularize the \emph{prediction space} directly. A flexible latent
function (e.g., gradient boosting) is followed by a supervised
quantization operator that produces a discrete, auditable score ladder
with monotone, operationally meaningful prediction levels. This
output-space bottleneck suppresses sub-bin fluctuations, enhances
stability, and yields structures compatible with traditional governance
frameworks while retaining the modeling power of modern ensembles.

Across multiple public datasets, QPMs match the predictive performance
of strong Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM) baselines in common
evaluation metrics such as AUC, KS, and MSE—even under relatively
coarse quantization. At the same time, QPM updates exhibit significantly
lower churn than independent retraining of GBMs. With fixed bin edges,
Anchored Calibration guarantees \emph{zero} rank-order churn, enabling
models to incorporate new information without disrupting downstream
decision logic or recalibration procedures.

These results support three central conclusions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Predictive Parity.}  
    Coarse quantization (on the order of tens of levels) is sufficient to
    match the performance of continuous-output GBMs on tabular data.

    \item \textbf{Stability.}  
    QPM fine-tuning with fixed bin edges yields 2--4$\times$ reductions
    in prediction churn across model updates, with strict zero-churn
    guarantees under calibration-only adjustments.

    \item \textbf{Governance Alignment.}  
    The discrete score ladders produced by QPMs offer stable,
    interpretable structures compatible with governance and audit
    requirements, while preserving the flexibility of modern ensemble
    methods.
\end{enumerate}

More broadly, QPMs demonstrate that the output space is a powerful but
underexplored axis for regularization. They provide a principled middle
ground between fully differentiable machine learning models and
handcrafted risk scorecards, unifying the statistical advantages of
modern ensembles with the stability needs of real-world decision
systems.

\paragraph{Future Work.}
Several promising extensions arise from this formulation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multi-Class and Ordinal Quantization.}  
    Extending QPMs to multi-class or ordinal targets would broaden their
    applicability to domains such as fraud detection, collections,
    medical-risk staging, and behavioral modeling.

    \item \textbf{Reinforcement Learning.}  
    Quantized value functions or discretized policy outputs may stabilize
    policy iteration in settings with noisy rewards or non-stationary
    environments.

    \item \textbf{Differentiable Quantizers.}  
    Soft or annealed quantizers could connect QPMs to differentiable
    architectures and straight-through gradient estimators, enabling
    end-to-end training.

    \item \textbf{Dynamic Bin Evolution.}  
    Bayesian or online-learning variants may support gradual bin-edge
    adaptation while preserving stability guarantees or bounded churn.
\end{itemize}

In summary, QPMs offer a practical and principled approach that unifies
the predictive strengths of modern machine learning with the stability
and interpretability required for responsible deployment. This work
highlights output-space regularization as a central design dimension in
predictive modeling and invites further exploration of its theoretical
and applied implications.

\section{Proof of Zero-Churn Property}
\label{app:zero-churn}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

Let $B(F(x))$ denote the bin index assigned to latent score $F(x)$ under
fixed edges $\{b_1,\ldots,b_{K+1}\}$. Anchored Calibration updates only
the bin representatives $\mu_k$ via:
\[
\mu_k^{\text{new}}
=
\alpha \,\mu_k^{\text{old}}
+
(1-\alpha)\,\mu_k^{\text{obs}},
\qquad \alpha\in[0,1].
\]

\begin{proposition}[Zero Rank-Order Churn]
If bin edges remain fixed, then Anchored Calibration preserves rank
ordering exactly:
\[
F(x_i) < F(x_j)
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
Q_{\text{new}}(F(x_i)) \le Q_{\text{new}}(F(x_j)),
\]
with equality only when both lie in the same bin.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since bin edges are fixed, any point $x$ remains assigned to the same 
bin under the update. Therefore:
\[
Q_{\text{new}}(F(x)) = \mu^{\text{new}}_{B(F(x))}.
\]
If $F(x_i) < F(x_j)$, then $B(F(x_i)) \le B(F(x_j))$. Because the mapping 
$k\mapsto\mu^{\text{new}}_k$ is monotone (as convex combinations of 
monotone sequences), the stated inequality follows.
\end{proof}


%--------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Variance Reduction Under Quantization}
\label{app:variance}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

Let $Z = F(X)$ denote the latent score random variable and define the
quantized prediction:
\[
\hat{Y}_Q = \mathbb{E}[Z \mid B(Z)].
\]

\begin{proposition}[Variance Reduction]
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\hat{Y}_Q) \;\le\; \mathrm{Var}(Z).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Apply the law of total variance:
\[
\mathrm{Var}(Z)
=
\mathbb{E}\!\left[\mathrm{Var}(Z\mid B(Z))\right]
+\mathrm{Var}\!\left(\mathbb{E}[Z\mid B(Z)]\right),
\]
and recognize the second term as $\mathrm{Var}(\hat{Y}_Q)$.
\end{proof}

\section{Statistical Tests for AUC Comparison}

\paragraph{Bootstrap Confidence Intervals.}
AUC confidence intervals were estimated using 2000 bootstrap resamples
with patient-level sampling and bias-corrected accelerated (BCa)
intervals. This approach is nonparametric and robust to moderate class
imbalance.

\paragraph{DeLong Test.}
To assess statistical significance in AUC differences between correlated
ROC curves, we applied the DeLong method, which provides an exact,
distribution-free estimate of the covariance of AUC estimators under
paired predictions. Given AUCs $A_1$ and $A_2$, the test statistic is
\[
Z = \frac{A_1 - A_2}{\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(A_1) + \mathrm{Var}(A_2) - 
2\,\mathrm{Cov}(A_1, A_2)}},
\]
with two-sided $p$-value computed under the standard normal
approximation.
